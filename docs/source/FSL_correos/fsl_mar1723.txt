Hi James,

All of the wrapper functions accept a "log" parameter, allowing you to control how the standard output/error streams are handled. This parameter is passed to the run function, and its meaning is documented here: https://open.win.ox.ac.uk/pages/fsl/fslpy/fsl.utils.run.html#fsl.utils.run.run

For example, if you want to stop the standard output/error of the underlying command being piped to the standard output/error of your process, you can set tee=False, e.g.:

bedpost.xfibres(‘data, mask,bvecs,bvals’, log={'tee':False})

Paul

fslview has command line interface to open images with certain intensity range and color and overlays (order)

On Mon, Mar 23, 2020 at 5:30 PM Shaun Warrington <Shaun.Warrington@nottingham.ac.uk> wrote:
Yes, the FA extraction process is basically the same depending on how it’s run, etc.. XTRACT is basically a wrapper around probtrackx: it calls probtrackx with pre-defined anatomical constraints and applies some further processing steps, e.g. for the reverse-seeding. So, you can treat the outputs very similarly.

There are many zeros in the images, mostly in the non-brain regions. And after thresholding, there will be many more zeros again - values less than the threshold value have been set as zero but will still be included in the “-m” mean calculation. Using the non-zero “-M” option is best in this case.

Regarding your AF waytotals, I generally find lower waytotals for the tractsInv run - the scale of the difference in this case may be related to data quality. Best to check the general appearance of the tract to make sure it has worked as expected.

On Mon, Mar 23, 2020 at 5:00 PM Shaun Warrington <Shaun.Warrington@nottingham.ac.uk> wrote:
Hi,

You’ll want to use the densityNorm.nii.gz file. With that, you can apply a consistent and meaningful threshold to remove the very low probabilities.

It is possible to have large differences between the waytotals as the size of the seed and target masks may be very different depending on the tract/protocol. Out of interest, which tract are you reporting here?

To get FA, you can now threshold and binarise the densityNorm files using fslmaths to get a tract mask. Typical threshold values are 0.001 or 0.005 - best to apply threshold and inspect the mask to see whether it is fit for your purposes. e.g. fslmaths densityNorm -thr 0.001 -bin densityNorm_bin

Once you have a binary mask, you can multiply it by the subject’s FA map to get a tract specific distribution of FA values. Depending on how you have run XTRACT, you may need to transform the tract mask (or densityNorm) to the subject’s native space prior to multiplication (assuming the FA map is in the subject’s native space).

Thanks,
Shaun.

On Mon, Mar 23, 2020 at 4:06 PM Shaun Warrington <Shaun.Warrington@nottingham.ac.uk> wrote:
Hi Nabin,

1. For some of the tracts, we use a "reverse-seeding” approach. i.e. we run tractography with a seed and target, then we repeat tractography after switching the seed and target (target becomes seed and seed becomes target). The second run is stored in the tractsINV folder. After running both, the results are summed for the final probability distributions. So, you want to use the files in the parent/tract directory.

 2. The "density.nii.gz" file is the fibre probability distribution. The "density_lenths.nii.gz" file contains the fibre lengths, each voxel is the average streamline length - this is the "-ompl" probtrackx option. The "densityNorm.nii.gz" is the waytotal normalised fibre probability distribution. Just the "density.nii.gz" divided by the total number of valid streamlines. So fslstats mean will give you the mean of the probability distribution, the mean of the length of fibres and the mean of the normalised probability distribution respectively.

Hope that helps!

Thanks,
Shaun.
